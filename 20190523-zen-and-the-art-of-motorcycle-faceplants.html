Banks and Isham experimented by delaying feedback on actions --- template:title-white background-image: url({{ site.baseurl }}/images/clock.gif) background-size: auto ??? They set up a computer screen with a clock that would complete two revolutions in 5.2 seconds And a button inside a box that participants were asked to press at any moment within the second revolution of the clock. A beep sounded at 5, 20, 40 or 60 ms after the button was pressed. Electrodes registered the actual neural signal to press the button. And they asked participants to point to the time on the clock when they decided to press the button. --- template: list-dark # So now they have four moments in time measured: -- ## The time the electrodes registered a signal -- ## The time the button was pressed -- ## The time the beep sounded -- ## The time the participants reported they pressed the button ??? What Banks and Isham found was that when they increased the beep delay, the difference between participants' reported button press time and actual button press time increased. For the 20, 40 and 60 ms delays, the reported button press time actually came after the electrode signal. --- template: title-dark # Ergo, free will? ??? This suggests that your judgment of when you made a decision is a retrospective estimate and is not based on an actual awareness of the neural activity that causes the movement. And it's one of many experiments trying to address the concept of free will. --- # Think of a city ??? Think of a city, any city in the world. Why did you name this city?
